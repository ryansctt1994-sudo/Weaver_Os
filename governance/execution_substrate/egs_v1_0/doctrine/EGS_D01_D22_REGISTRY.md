# EGS v1.0 Doctrine Registry

## I. Canonical Core Doctrine

### D01 — Governed Compiler Principle
Information may be open. Execution must be permissioned. The machine that converts digital designs into physical reality is not a neutral printer; it is a governed compiler.

### D02 — Bits-to-Atoms Boundary
The critical governance surface is the conversion boundary: information -> design -> physical instantiation. Risk concentrates where symbolic representations become matter.

### D03 — Physical Execution Supremacy
Primary governance focus belongs on synthesis systems, fabrication systems, autonomous laboratory infrastructure, programmable matter infrastructure, and other physical execution endpoints.

## II. Governance Trilemma

### D04 — Open-Model Trilemma
Open models, privacy, and design monitoring cannot all hold at maximum strength. At most two can be strongly preserved. Local models reduce visibility into design generation.

## III. Execution Governance Architecture

### D05 — Permissioned Execution Doctrine
Execution requires identity validation, device attestation, authorization token, facility validation, reagent validation, and audit trail. No quorum means no execution.

### D06 — Execution Tier Framework
Tier 0: known-safe execution. Tier 1: reviewable execution. Tier 2: escalated execution in a secure facility. Tier 3: contained empirical execution. Tier 4: national-review or prohibited execution.

### D07 — Escalation Quorum Principle
Uncertainty does not grant authority. Uncertainty requires escalation quorum. Novel designs are escalated upward rather than automatically banned or locally executed.

## IV. Screening Architecture

### D08 — Dual-Engine Screening
Engine A detects known hazards, including known pathogens, toxins, regulated sequences, and known dangerous fragments. Engine B estimates functional risk, including pathogenic potential, toxicity, persistence, weaponization affordance, environmental impact, and uncertainty.

### D09 — Functional over Homology
Database matching alone is insufficient. Future screening must evaluate function over similarity for novel biological and chemical designs.

## V. Authorization Infrastructure

### D10 — Socio-Technical Trust Stack
Execution authority emerges from cryptography, hardware, identity, institutions, supply chains, facility controls, audit systems, and revocation systems. No single component is sovereign.

### D11 — Context-Bound Execution Tokens
Execution tokens bind user, institution, project, facility, device, design, reagent lot, time window, and approval scope. Tokens are short-lived, non-transferable, revocable, and execution-specific.

### D12 — Trust-Bloc Reality
Global single-root governance is unlikely. Architecture must survive fragmented trust fabrics, including national, regional, institutional, and non-compliant ecosystems.

## VI. Hardware Security Doctrine

### D13 — Fail-Closed Execution
Unauthorized execution must not merely be denied. It must be physically interrupted.

### D14 — Synthesis State Machine
Canonical flow: Idle -> Authenticated -> Loaded -> Screened -> Armed -> Synthesizing -> Verified -> Released. Invalid transitions force Lockout -> Neutralize -> Purge -> Seal -> Audit -> Recertify.

### D15 — Matter Safety Invariant
No unpermissioned state may advance matter toward a recoverable hazardous product.

## VII. Supply Chain Governance

### D16 — Reagent Control Layer
Governance includes reagents, enzymes, precursors, catalysts, cartridges, and specialized consumables. Hardware alone is insufficient.

### D17 — Execution Chain Governance
Govern design, hardware, supply chain, facility, and authorization together rather than any single layer alone.

## VIII. Bio vs Nano Distinction

### D18 — Biological Screening Advantage
Biology has a finite symbolic alphabet, standardized encoding, and known translation systems, which enables sequence-based screening.

### D19 — Nanotechnology Screening Limitation
Nanotechnology requires process screening, property screening, reaction-pathway analysis, and materials-risk modeling. Direct analogy to DNA screening is invalid.

## IX. Strategic Conclusions

### D20 — Concentration vs Diffusion Law
Governance effectiveness depends on whether critical capability remains centralized or becomes decentralized.

### D21 — Controlled Escalation Doctrine
High-risk novelty should route local device -> institutional sandbox -> contained facility -> national review, rather than local device -> gray market.

### D22 — Final Canonical Principle
Information can remain open. Design can remain plural. Matter-compilation must be tiered, permissioned, auditable, and physically interruptible. Uncertainty does not deny science; it denies unsupervised execution.
